Chapter 11 — Introduction

Why Equalization? — Delay Spread and ISI

Before we study any equalizer design, we need to deeply understand the problem being solved. This section builds the story from the very beginning.

Step 1 — The Problem That Starts Everything: Multipath

In wireless communication, a signal transmitted from an antenna does not travel to the receiver through only one path. Instead, it travels through many paths because it reflects from buildings, walls, trees, vehicles, and the ground.

This is called multipath propagation. Because each path has a different length, the copies of the signal arrive at the receiver at different times.

Transmitter
     |
     ├──── direct path ──────────────────► Receiver
     |
     └── reflection 1 (longer path) ─────► Receiver (delayed)
     |
     └── reflection 2 (even longer) ──────► Receiver (more delayed)

Each copy arrives at a different time!
Multipath propagation — the root cause of all ISI problems

The spread between these arrival times is called the delay spread of the channel.

Step 2 — What Delay Spread Does to Symbols: ISI

Digital communication sends symbols one after another, each lasting a duration called the symbol time Ts. Because of delay spread, the delayed copy of Symbol 1 may still be arriving when Symbol 2 is already being transmitted. At the receiver:

Transmitted:  [Symbol 1]  [Symbol 2]  [Symbol 3]
                   ↓           ↓           ↓
At Receiver:  [Symbol 1][delayed1+Symbol2][delayed2+Symbol3]
                         ↑↑↑
                    These overlap! Receiver gets confused.
Intersymbol Interference (ISI) — previous symbols spill into the current one

Definition — Intersymbol Interference (ISI)

ISI occurs when previous symbols interfere with the current symbol due to channel delay spread. In simple terms: one symbol "spills" into the next.

Step 3 — When Does ISI Become Serious?

ISI becomes problematic when the symbol time and the delay spread are on the same order:

Ts ≈ σ_Tm Ts = symbol duration · σ_Tm = RMS delay spread of the channel
ConditionEffectISI?
Ts ≫ σ_TmSymbol lasts much longer than delay spread — delayed copies finish before next symbol beginsNegligible
Ts ≈ σ_TmDelayed signals overlap the next symbol significantlySevere

Step 4 — Why High Data Rate Systems Have More ISI

📡
The key relationship: Symbol time and data rate are inversely related: Ts = 1/Rs. If data rate increases, symbol duration shrinks. If symbols become very short, the delay spread easily overlaps multiple symbols. This is why the textbook says: "Mitigating delay spread is one of the most challenging hurdles for high-speed wireless data systems."
Real-World Examples from the Textbook
Cordless Phones ✓

Indoor, small delay spread, low data rate. Ts ≫ σ_Tm → ISI negligible. Equalization NOT needed.

IS-136 Digital Cellular ✗

Outdoor, buildings, large delay spread. σ_Tm ≈ Ts → severe ISI. Equalization REQUIRED.

Step 5 — The Solution: Equalization

Equalization is defined as: any signal processing technique used at the receiver to alleviate the ISI problem caused by delay spread.

Think of the wireless channel as a distorting filter. The equalizer applies another filter to reverse that distortion:

Clean symbols
→
Channel (distorts)
→
Equalizer (un-distorts)
→
Recovered symbols

Step 6 — Signal Processing at the Transmitter (Alternative Approaches)

Instead of fixing the problem at the receiver, we can also design the transmitted signal more cleverly:

TechniqueIdeaWhere studied
EqualizationReceiver-side filter to undo channel distortionChapter 11 (this chapter)
Multicarrier (OFDM)Split data into many slow streams → longer symbol time → less ISIChapter 12
Spread SpectrumSpread signal across wide bandwidth → multipath effects less harmfulChapter 13

Step 7 — The New Problem: Noise Enhancement

Critical trade-off: The received signal contains signal + noise. When the equalizer processes the received signal to remove ISI, it processes the noise too — and can amplify it. This is called noise enhancement. Equalizer design must always balance ISI removal against noise amplification.

Step 8 — The Channel Must Be Learned: Training and Tracking

An equalizer must know how the channel distorts the signal (the channel impulse or frequency response). But wireless channels change over time due to user movement and environment changes.

Training

Transmitter sends known symbols. Receiver compares sent vs received to estimate the channel. Like a calibration step.

Tracking

After training, the equalizer continuously updates its channel estimate as the channel changes.

When the equalizer automatically updates itself based on changing channel conditions, it is called Adaptive Equalization.

Step 9 — Where Is the Equalizer Implemented?

An equalizer can operate at baseband, carrier frequency, or intermediate frequency (IF). Most modern equalizers are implemented digitally after A/D conversion because such filters are:

  • Small and cheap in hardware
  • Easily tunable (just change tap weights in software)
  • Very power efficient
  • Programmable — same hardware, different algorithms
Practice — §11 Introduction
  1. A system operates outdoors where σ_Tm = 5 μs. If the symbol rate is 200 kbps, does equalization seem necessary? What about at 10 Mbps?
  2. Explain the difference between equalizer training and equalizer tracking. When would tracking be harder than training?
  3. Why is OFDM an alternative to equalization for handling ISI? What property of OFDM reduces ISI?

1. At 200 kbps: Ts = 1/200,000 = 5 μs. σ_Tm = Ts → ISI is significant, equalization likely needed. At 10 Mbps: Ts = 0.1 μs ≪ σ_Tm = 5 μs → severe ISI, equalization absolutely required (delay spread is 50× longer than symbol time).

2. Training: transmitter sends pilot (known) symbols; receiver uses the known sequence to estimate H once. Tracking: after training, the channel may change (user moves, environment changes) so the equalizer must keep updating its estimate using decision-directed feedback. Tracking is harder when the channel changes faster than the equalizer can update (high mobility or high Doppler).

3. OFDM splits one high-speed data stream into many parallel low-speed streams transmitted on different subcarriers. Each subcarrier has a much longer symbol time (N times longer for N subcarriers). Since Ts ≫ σ_Tm per subcarrier, each experiences flat fading (no ISI) rather than frequency-selective fading. A simple one-tap equalizer per subcarrier is then sufficient instead of a complex time-domain equalizer.

Section 11.1

Equalizer Noise Enhancement

The goal of equalization is to remove ISI — but trying too hard creates a new problem. Removing ISI can amplify noise so severely that the system performs worse than with ISI.

Step 1 — The Received Signal Model

The transmitted signal s(t) passes through a channel with frequency response H(f). At the receiver, white Gaussian noise n(t) is added. So the received signal in the frequency domain is:

Y(f) = S(f)·H(f) + N(f) Received = (signal distorted by channel) + noise · N(f) = white noise with PSD N₀/2

If the bandwidth of s(t) is B, the noise power within the signal bandwidth is:

Noise power (before equalization) = N₀ · B Total noise power = PSD × bandwidth = (N₀/2) × 2B = N₀B (two-sided to one-sided)

Step 2 — The Ideal (Perfect) Equalizer

To completely remove ISI, we want to undo the channel distortion. If the channel applies H(f), we design an equalizer that applies the inverse:

H_eq(f) = 1 / H(f) Channel inverse equalizer — perfectly cancels H(f) since H(f) × (1/H(f)) = 1

Step 3 — What Happens After Equalization

Multiply the received signal Y(f) by the equalizer:

[S(f)·H(f) + N(f)] × (1/H(f))

= S(f)·H(f)/H(f) + N(f)/H(f)

= S(f) + N'(f)    where N'(f) = N(f)/H(f)Eq. (11.1) — ISI is completely removed ✓ but noise is modified!

The new noise N'(f) is now colored noise with power spectral density:

PSD of N'(f) = (N₀/2) / |H(f)|²
🎨
Why "colored" noise? Original noise N(f) had equal power at all frequencies — that's white noise. After dividing by H(f), the noise power varies with frequency — different "colors" at different frequencies. Where |H(f)| is small, the noise power 1/|H(f)|² becomes large.

Step 4 — The Spectral Null Disaster

Suppose the channel has a spectral null at some frequency f₀:

H(f₀) = 0 for some f₀ within signal bandwidth A spectral null means the channel completely kills that frequency

Then the equalizer at that frequency becomes:

H_eq(f₀) = 1/H(f₀) = 1/0 = ∞
Catastrophe: The equalizer must amplify f₀ infinitely to "restore" it. But the original signal at f₀ is zero — there's nothing to restore. What gets amplified to infinity? Noise. The noise power becomes infinite. Even without a full spectral null, if H(f) is just very small at some frequency, the equalizer amplifies noise enormously there.

Step 5 — The Fundamental Trade-off

★ The core principle of all equalizer design: The goal of equalization is NOT simply to remove all ISI. It is to balance ISI mitigation with maximising the SNR of the post-equalization signal. Complete channel inversion = zero ISI but potentially infinite noise → terrible performance.
Equalizer TypeISI removalNoise enhancementPractical performance
Perfect channel inverseCompletePotentially infiniteCan be terrible
Linear (partial inversion)GoodSignificantModerate
Nonlinear (DFE)GoodMuch lessBetter
MLSEOptimalDoes not invertBest
Example 11.1 — How Bad Can Noise Enhancement Get? (Textbook)

Channel: H(f) = 1/√|f| for |f| < B, B = 30 kHz. Noise PSD = N₀/2.

Without equalization: Noise power = N₀·B = N₀ × 30,000 = 3×10⁴·N₀

With channel-inverse equalizer: H_eq(f) = 1/H(f) = √|f|. New noise PSD = (N₀/2)·|H_eq(f)|² = (N₀/2)·|f|.

Total noise power = ∫₋B^B (N₀/2)·|f| df = (N₀/2)·B² = (N₀/2)·(30000)² = 4.5×10⁸·N₀

Noise increased by more than 10,000× (four orders of magnitude)! The equalizer amplified noise by a factor of 4.5×10⁸ / 3×10⁴ = 15,000. This system would perform catastrophically worse with the equalizer than without it — despite having zero ISI.
Practice — §11.1
  1. A channel has H(f) = 0.1 at frequency f = 5 kHz (within signal bandwidth). What is the noise amplification factor at that frequency from a channel-inverse equalizer?
  2. Explain why nonlinear equalizers (like DFE) suffer less from noise enhancement than linear equalizers. What is the key difference in their approach?
  3. If a channel has a spectral null at f₀ = 3 kHz, a channel-inverse equalizer would amplify noise to infinity there. What practical solutions exist for this problem?

1. H_eq(f) = 1/0.1 = 10. Noise power amplification = |H_eq|² = 100 (20 dB). The equalizer amplifies noise 100× at that frequency.

2. Linear equalizers work by approximately inverting H(f). Where H(f) is small, the inverse is large → noise amplification. Nonlinear equalizers (like DFE) don't directly invert H(f). Instead they use already-decided symbols to cancel known ISI. They don't need to amplify the received signal at weakened frequencies — they subtract the known ISI contribution directly, avoiding the noise amplification problem.

3. Practical solutions: (a) MMSE equalizer — instead of 1/H(f), use H*(f)/(|H(f)|² + N₀/signal_power), which "regularises" the inversion and prevents infinite amplification. (b) Decision Feedback Equalizer — avoids inversion entirely. (c) Constrain equalizer taps to limit maximum gain at any frequency. (d) Diversity techniques to avoid deep fades causing spectral nulls.

Section 11.2

Equalizer Types

There are many kinds of equalizers. This section gives the full map — what they are, how they differ, and the key trade-offs between them.

The Big Picture — Two Main Categories

Linear Equalizers

Use a linear filter. Simplest to implement and understand. Output is a weighted sum of received samples. Trade-off: suffer more noise enhancement than nonlinear methods.

Nonlinear Equalizers

Use additional processing (decision feedback, sequence estimation). More complex but much less noise enhancement. Better BER performance.

Linear Equalizers — Details

A linear equalizer uses a linear filter: the output is simply a weighted sum of delayed received samples:

output = w₀·y(k) + w₁·y(k−1) + w₂·y(k−2) + ··· y(k) = received sample · wᵢ = tap weights (tunable)

They are: ✓ simple to implement, ✓ easy to understand, ✓ computationally light. But: they try to invert the channel → significant noise enhancement.

DFE — Decision Feedback Equalizer

DFE is the most widely used nonlinear equalizer. Instead of inverting the channel, it:

  1. Detects (decides) previous symbols
  2. Uses those decisions to subtract their interference from the current sample
Received signal
       │
 Forward filter (removes pre-cursor ISI)
       │
 Decision device → detected symbol
       │                   │
       │          Feedback filter
       │      (subtracts post-cursor ISI)
       └─────────────────────┘
DFE structure — forward filter + feedback loop through decision device
DFE Advantages

Much less noise enhancement than linear equalizers. Good BER performance. Moderate complexity — widely used in practice.

DFE Weakness: Error Propagation

If a symbol is detected incorrectly, that wrong value is fed back → creates new interference → next symbol may also be wrong → chain reaction. Especially bad at low SNR.

MLSE — Maximum Likelihood Sequence Estimation

MLSE is the optimal equalizer. Instead of detecting symbols one-by-one, MLSE detects the entire sequence of symbols. It finds the sequence that most likely produced the received signal, considering ISI + noise + channel behaviour together.

Result: The best possible BER — it cannot be beaten by any other equalizer.

However: complexity grows exponentially with delay spread length. If channel length = L and alphabet size = M, complexity = M^L. This makes MLSE impractical for most real channels. It is used as a performance benchmark — other equalizers are compared against MLSE BER.

Second Classification: SBS vs Sequence Estimators

Sequence Estimators (SE)Detects entire sequences of symbols — ISI is part of the estimation processMLSE

Equalizer Structures

Transversal (Tapped Delay Line)

N−1 delay elements + N tunable weights. Simple, flexible, easy to implement digitally. Used in most real systems. This chapter focuses on transversal.

Lattice Filter

More complex recursive structure. Better numerical stability and faster convergence. More flexible length changes. Higher complexity — less common in practice.

Practice — §11.2
  1. Compare ZF linear equalizer vs DFE in terms of noise enhancement and error propagation. Under what channel conditions would you prefer each?
  2. Why is MLSE considered optimal? Why is it usually not used in practical systems?
  3. A channel has delay spread equivalent to L=5 symbol periods. Alphabet size M=4 (QPSK). What is the MLSE complexity? At what point does this become impractical?

1. ZF: inverts channel completely → potentially large noise enhancement, especially at attenuated frequencies. No error propagation (no feedback). Prefer when SNR is high and channel is well-behaved. DFE: uses decisions to cancel ISI — much less noise enhancement. But error propagation occurs at low SNR when decisions are wrong. Prefer DFE at moderate-high SNR for typical wireless channels.

2. MLSE is optimal because it uses all available information — it considers the entire sequence jointly with the channel model and noise statistics, not just isolated symbols. In practice: MLSE complexity = M^L. For L=10 taps and M=4: 4^10 = ~1 million states to evaluate per received sample, for every sample in real time. For a 1 Mbps system processing 10^6 samples/second, this is 10^12 operations/second — far beyond real-time capability.

3. MLSE complexity = M^L = 4^5 = 1024 states. For comparison: L=10 → 4^10 ≈ 1 million. L=15 → 4^15 ≈ 10^9. Practical limit is usually L ≤ 5–6 for MLSE. Beyond that, reduced-state MLSE or DFE is used instead.

Section 11.3

Folded Spectrum and ISI-Free Transmission

Before designing equalizers, we ask: when is a system naturally free from ISI? This section derives the exact condition — in both time and frequency domains. It leads to the famous Nyquist criterion.

Step 1 — The End-to-End System Model

The full signal path from transmitter to receiver (equivalent lowpass representation) looks like this:

→
Channel c(t)
+n(t)

→
Sampler (A/D)
→
Equalizer
→
Decision device

Each Block Explained

BlockSymbolPurposeControlled by
Input symbolsd_kInformation to send—

Channelc(t)Introduces multipath, fading, delay spreadNature — NOT controllable ✗

Combined Channel Response

The combined channel = pulse shape + physical channel: h(t) = g(t) * c(t). The composite response including matched filter:

f(t) ≜ g(t) * c(t) * g*_m(−t) Eq. (11.2) — f(t) is the total system response from symbol input to equalizer input · * = convolution

Why Not Always Match to h(t)?

Ideally, g_m(t) = h(t) maximises SNR. But since c(t) changes over time and analog filters aren't easily tunable, designers often set g_m(t) = g(t) (matched to the pulse only). This is suboptimal when c(t) ≠ δ(t) — it can cause sensitivity to timing errors. One solution: fractionally spaced equalization — sample at rate faster than 1/Ts, then let the digital equalizer handle the mismatch.

Step 2 — The Discrete-Time Signal y[n]

The matched filter output: y(t) = Σ_k d_k · f(t − kTs) + n_g(t) (Eq. 11.3)

Sampling at rate 1/Ts gives the discrete-time signal:

y[n] = d_n·f[0] + Σ_{k≠n} d_k·f[n−k] + ν[n] Eq. (11.4) — Three terms: desired symbol, ISI, noise · f[n] = f(nTs) = samples of f(t)
TermExpressionWhat it is
1std_n · f[0]The desired symbol we want to detect
2ndΣ_{k≠n} d_k · f[n−k]ISI — interference from all other symbols
3rdν[n]Sampled noise

Step 3 — The Condition for Zero ISI

ISI disappears when the second term = 0. This requires f[n−k] = 0 for all k ≠ n, i.e.:

f[k] = δ[k] · f[0] ISI-free time-domain condition: f[0] is nonzero, f[k] = 0 for all k ≠ 0

When this holds, y[n] = d_n · f[0] + ν[n] — only the desired symbol plus noise. No ISI!

Ideal f[k] for zero ISI:

... 0   0   f[0]   0   0   0 ...
           (only one nonzero sample)

Impulse response samples at Ts, 2Ts, 3Ts... must ALL be zero!

Step 4 — The Folded Spectrum

The frequency-domain equivalent of the ISI-free condition is much more intuitive to work with:

F̃(f) ≜ (1/Ts) · Σ_{n=−∞}^{∞} F(f + n/Ts) Eq. (11.5) — The Folded Spectrum · F(f) = Fourier transform of f(t) · periodic with period 1/Ts
📂
Why "folded"? The spectrum F(f) is shifted by multiples of 1/Ts and added together — like folding a piece of paper repeatedly. The result is periodic with period 1/Ts.

The Key Theorem: Flat Folded Spectrum ↔ No ISI

A transmission is ISI-free if and only if the folded spectrum is flat (constant):

F̃(f) = f[0] (constant) ↔ f[k] = δ[k]·f[0]

This is the Nyquist ISI-free condition in the frequency domain. It forms the foundation of all Nyquist pulse shaping (raised cosine, root raised cosine filters).

Example 11.2 — Sinc Pulse Is ISI-Free (Textbook)

Given: f(t) = sinc(t/Ts). Is this ISI-free?

Step 1 — Find F(f): Using the Fourier transform pair sinc(t) ↔ rect(f) and the time-scaling property:

F(f) = Ts · rect(f·Ts)Rectangle of height Ts, width 1/Ts, centred at f=0

Explicitly (applying the rect definition):

F(f) = Ts for |f| < 0.5/Ts · F(f) = Ts/2 for |f| = 0.5/Ts · F(f) = 0 for |f| > 0.5/Ts The 0.5Ts boundary value comes from rect(x)=1/2 at |x|=1/2 (Dirichlet convention)

Step 2 — Compute folded spectrum:

F(f) has width exactly 1/Ts and the shifts are by 1/Ts — so shifted copies tile the frequency axis perfectly with no gaps and no overlaps. At any frequency f, exactly ONE shifted rectangle contributes value Ts:

F̃(f) = (1/Ts) × Ts = 1 = constant ✓Folded spectrum is flat → system is ISI-free!

Time-domain verification: f(nTs) = sinc(n) = 1 for n=0, 0 for n≠0 → f[k] = δ[k] ✓

Practice — §11.3 Folded Spectrum
  1. A system has combined response f(t) = sinc(t/Ts)·cos(πt/Ts). Without computing the full Fourier transform, explain why this might NOT be ISI-free. (Hint: what does multiplication by cosine do in frequency domain?)
  2. Explain in simple terms: why does a rectangular frequency spectrum (bandlimited to 1/(2Ts)) lead to zero ISI at all sampling instants?
  3. A channel response f(t) has F(f) = 2Ts·rect(2f·Ts) (twice as wide). Compute the folded spectrum and determine if the system is ISI-free.

1. Multiplying by cos(πt/Ts) in time = convolution with two shifted delta functions in frequency. F_new(f) = (1/2)[F(f − 1/(2Ts)) + F(f + 1/(2Ts))]. This shifts the rectangular spectrum to centre at ±1/(2Ts). Now the shifted copies in the folded spectrum may not tile perfectly — they likely overlap or have gaps. If they don't tile to a constant, the folded spectrum isn't flat → ISI exists.

2. If F(f) is a rectangle of width 1/Ts, then when we shift it by 1/Ts, 2/Ts, 3/Ts... each shifted copy exactly fills the gap left by the previous one. One copy covers [-1/(2Ts), 1/(2Ts)], the next covers [1/(2Ts), 3/(2Ts)], etc. Their sum is constant everywhere — a perfect tiling of the frequency axis. This flatness means each symbol's contribution at other sampling instants is zero (Nyquist criterion).

3. F(f) = 2Ts·rect(2fTs) → width = 1/Ts (same as before, just shifted scaling). At any f, computing F̃(f) = (1/Ts)·ΣF(f+n/Ts): Each shifted copy has height 2Ts and width 1/(2Ts)·2 = 1/Ts... actually F(f) = 2Ts for |f| < 1/(4Ts). Width = 1/(2Ts). Shifts are by 1/Ts. Since 1/(2Ts) < 1/Ts, there are gaps between copies → F̃(f) is NOT constant (= 2Ts·(1/(2Ts))/(1/Ts) ×(1/Ts) = ... need numerical check). Width 1/(2Ts) < shift 1/Ts → gaps → NOT ISI-free.

Deep Dive

Understanding sinc, rect, and Why sinc(n) = 0

These are the most frequently asked follow-up questions about Section 11.3. Every step explained clearly.

Why sinc(n) = 0 for n ≠ 0

The sinc function is defined as:

sinc(x) = sin(πx) / (πx)

Substitute x = n (an integer):

sinc(n) = sin(πn) / (πn)

Numerator: sin(πn) = 0 for ALL integers n. This is because sin(kπ) = 0 for any integer k.

Examples: sin(π) = 0, sin(2π) = 0, sin(3π) = 0, sin(−2π) = 0.

Denominator: πn ≠ 0 when n ≠ 0.

So: sinc(n) = 0/πn = 0 for all n ≠ 0. ✓

What About n = 0?

sinc(0) = lim_{x→0} sin(πx)/(πx) = 1 Using L'Hôpital's rule or the standard limit lim_{θ→0} sin(θ)/θ = 1 
sinc(n) values:
  n = -3: sinc(-3) = 0
  n = -2: sinc(-2) = 0
  n = -1: sinc(-1) = 0
  n =  0: sinc( 0) = 1  ← only nonzero value
  n =  1: sinc( 1) = 0
  n =  2: sinc( 2) = 0
  n =  3: sinc( 3) = 0
The sinc function is zero at ALL integer sampling instants except n=0
★ Why this eliminates ISI: If f(t) = sinc(t/Ts), then at each sampling instant nTs, the pulse from symbol k contributes f((n−k)Ts) = sinc(n−k) = 0 whenever n≠k. So every other symbol contributes ZERO at the sampling instant of symbol k. No ISI!
📊
Important clarification: The sinc graph is NOT zero everywhere! It oscillates with side lobes. It is only zero at the specific integer sampling points. This is the key trick — the sampling instants happen to align perfectly with the zeros.

How the Fourier Transform of sinc(t/Ts) Gives rect(fTs)

Step 1 — Start from the known pair

sinc(t) ↔ rect(f) Standard Fourier transform pair — sinc in time, rectangle in frequency

Step 2 — Apply the time-scaling property

If x(t) ↔ X(f), then x(at) ↔ (1/|a|)·X(f/a). Here a = 1/Ts:

sinc(t/Ts) ↔ Ts · rect(f·Ts) Scaling by 1/Ts in time → multiply by Ts and stretch rect by Ts in frequency

Step 3 — Unpack the rect definition

The rectangle function: rect(x) = 1 for |x| < 1/2, rect(x) = 1/2 for |x| = 1/2, rect(x) = 0 for |x| > 1/2. Substitute x = f·Ts:

ConditionTranslates toF(f) = Ts·rect(fTs)
|fTs| < 1/2|f| < 1/(2Ts) = 0.5/TsTs × 1 = Ts
|fTs| = 1/2|f| = 0.5/TsTs × 1/2 = Ts/2 = 0.5Ts
|fTs| > 1/2|f| > 0.5/TsTs × 0 = 0

The 0.5Ts at the boundary comes from rect(x) = 1/2 at its exact edge. In practice, this boundary point has zero measure and doesn't affect results — engineers usually just write F(f) = Ts for |f| < 0.5/Ts.

Why the Folded Spectrum of sinc(t/Ts) Becomes 1

The rectangle F(f) has height Ts and width 1/Ts. The folded spectrum shifts copies by 1/Ts:

F(f) copies at n·(1/Ts) for n = ..., -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, ...

|   Ts  |   Ts  |   Ts  |   Ts  |   Ts  |
|_______|_______|_______|_______|_______|
-3/2Ts -1/2Ts  1/2Ts  3/2Ts  5/2Ts

Each rectangle of height Ts and width 1/Ts.
They tile the axis perfectly — no gaps, no overlaps!

At any frequency f, exactly ONE shifted copy contributes: value = Ts. Multiplying by 1/Ts:

F̃(f) = (1/Ts) × Ts = 1 for all f
Section 11.4

Linear Equalizers

When the folded spectrum is not flat, we add an equalizer. This section designs two practical linear equalizers: the Zero-Forcing (ZF) equalizer and the MMSE equalizer.

Structure of a Linear Equalizer

A linear equalizer is implemented as a transversal filter with 2L+1 = N taps:

H_eq(z) = Σ_{i=−L}^{L} wᵢ · z^(−i) Eq. (11.9) · N = 2L+1 taps · wᵢ = tap weights · z^(−i) = i-step delay

Design Decisions for Any Linear Equalizer

  1. Tap weights {wᵢ} — what values do the taps take for a given channel?
  2. Update algorithm — how do tap weights adapt as the channel changes?

Ideal goal: choose wᵢ to minimise BER. In practice: BER minimisation is mathematically intractable, so we use indirect criteria that balance ISI removal and noise enhancement.

Causal equalizer: wᵢ = 0 for i < 0 (cannot use future samples — only past and present).

Section 11.4.1

Zero-Forcing (ZF) Equalizer

The simplest idea: completely invert the channel. All ISI disappears. But watch out — noise can explode.

The Signal Model in Z-domain

From Eq. (11.4), the received signal samples can be written in the z-domain as:

Y(z) = D(z)·F(z) + Ng(z) Eq. (11.10) · D(z) = transmitted symbols · F(z) = combined channel response · Ng(z) = noise

where F(z) = H(z)·G*_m(1/z*) combines the channel H(z) and matched filter response.

The ZF Design

To remove all ISI from F(z), we need an equalizer that inverts it exactly:

H_ZF(z) = 1 / F(z) Eq. (11.12) — Zero-forcing equalizer: discrete-time equivalent of the analog 1/H(f) equalizer

After applying ZF: output = D(z) + noise. All ISI removed ✓.

Noise After ZF Equalization

The power spectrum of noise at the ZF equalizer output:

N(z) = N₀ · |G*_m(1/z*)|² · |H_ZF(z)|² = N₀ / |H(z)|² Eq. (11.13) — Noise power spectrum after ZF equalization · Directly inversely proportional to |H(z)|²
The ZF noise problem: On frequency-selective fading channels, |H(z)| can be very small at some frequencies (deep fades). Then 1/|H(z)|² becomes very large → significant noise amplification. This is the fundamental weakness of the ZF equalizer.

Practical Issue: ZF May Not Be FIR

The ideal ZF equalizer H_ZF(z) = 1/F(z) may require an infinite number of taps (IIR filter). But real equalizers have finite taps (FIR). So we must approximate:

Method 1 — IIR Truncation

Expand 1/F(z) = Σcᵢz^(−i) as an infinite series, then set wᵢ = cᵢ for the available taps. Minimises distance between the ideal IIR and the FIR approximation.

Method 2 — Peak Distortion Minimisation

Find tap weights that minimise worst-case ISI (peak distortion). This is a convex optimisation problem solvable by steepest descent method.

Example 11.3 — Two-Tap ZF Equalizer (Textbook)

Channel: h(t) = e^(−t/τ) for t ≥ 0.

Step 1 — Sample the channel:

h[n] = e^(−nTs/τ) for n ≥ 0. So: h[0]=1, h[1]=e^(−Ts/τ), h[2]=e^(−2Ts/τ), ...

Step 2 — Find H(z):

H(z) = Σ_{n=0}^{∞} (e^(−Ts/τ) z^(−1))^n = 1/(1 − e^(−Ts/τ)z^(−1)) = z/(z − e^(−Ts/τ)) Geometric series: Σa^n = 1/(1−a) for |a| < 1

Step 3 — ZF Equalizer = 1/H(z):

H_ZF(z) = 1/H(z) = 1 − e^(−Ts/τ)·z^(−1) Only TWO taps needed for this exponential channel!

Step 4 — Read off tap weights:

TapWeightValue
w₀coefficient of z⁰1
w₁coefficient of z^(−1)e^(−Ts/τ)

The equalizer subtracts a delayed, attenuated copy of the received signal from itself — exactly cancelling the exponential tail of the channel!

Conversely as τ→0 (fast decay), w₁=e^(−Ts/τ)→0 — the equalizer reduces to just w₀=1 (no ISI correction needed since channel decays before next symbol).

Section 11.4.2

MMSE Equalizer — Balancing ISI and Noise

Instead of forcing ISI to zero (and letting noise explode), the MMSE equalizer finds the optimal balance between ISI removal and noise control.

What MMSE Minimises

Minimise: E[(d_n − d̂_n)²] Mean Squared Error between transmitted symbol d_n and equalizer output d̂_n · Balances both ISI and noise effects
⚖️
Why MMSE is better than ZF: ZF completely eliminates ISI but ignores noise. MMSE accepts a tiny bit of residual ISI in exchange for not amplifying noise explosively. The net result is a higher SNR at the equalizer output and better BER performance.

MMSE Equalizer Frequency Response

The optimal MMSE equalizer solution (derived via Wiener filter theory):

H_MMSE(f) = H*(f) / (|H(f)|² + N₀/Ps) MMSE equalizer · H*(f) = conjugate of channel · N₀ = noise PSD · Ps = signal power

Comparison: ZF vs MMSE

PropertyZFMMSE
Design criterionForce all ISI to zeroMinimise mean squared error
Equalizer form1/H(f)H*(f)/(|H(f)|²+N₀/Ps)
At high SNR—Approaches ZF (N₀/Ps → 0)
At low SNRNoise blows upSmoothly limits gain
Noise enhancementCan be very largeControlled — never infinite
Residual ISIZeroSmall but nonzero
BER performanceGood at high SNRBetter overall

Key Insight: N₀/Ps Acts as a Regulariser

In the MMSE formula, the term N₀/Ps in the denominator prevents the equalizer from becoming infinite, even when |H(f)| → 0. Compare:

  • ZF: 1/H(f) → ∞ as H(f) → 0
  • MMSE: H*(f)/(0 + N₀/Ps) = finite value even at spectral nulls

This is why MMSE is robust against spectral nulls while ZF is not.

Example — ZF vs MMSE at a Weak Frequency

Channel H(f₀) = 0.01 (very attenuated). Signal power Ps = 1, noise PSD N₀ = 0.001.

ZF at f₀: H_ZF = 1/0.01 = 100. Noise amplification = 100² = 10,000.

MMSE at f₀: H_MMSE = 0.01/(0.01² + 0.001) = 0.01/(0.0001 + 0.001) = 0.01/0.0011 ≈ 9.09.

MMSE amplification ≈ 9.09² ≈ 83 vs ZF = 10,000. MMSE reduces noise amplification by a factor of ~120 at this frequency!

At the cost of: small residual ISI. But the SNR gain far outweighs the residual ISI → better overall BER.

§7.7 Rappaport — DFE

Decision Feedback Equalizer (DFE)

The most widely used nonlinear equalizer. DFE avoids noise enhancement by using previously detected symbols to cancel ISI — instead of amplifying the signal to invert the channel.

Step 1 — Why DFE Was Invented

Recall the problem with linear equalizers:

  • ZF equalizer inverts the channel completely → large noise enhancement at attenuated frequencies
  • MMSE limits this but still applies a linear filter to the received signal

The key insight that leads to DFE:

💡
If we already know what previous symbols were, we can subtract their ISI contribution directly — without ever amplifying noise! We don't need to boost weak frequencies if we can just remove the ISI caused by past symbols using known values.

Step 2 — The ISI Problem, Revisited

From the sampled output equation: y[n] = d_n·f[0] + Σ_{k≠n} d_k·f[n−k] + ν[n]

The ISI term has two parts:

Pre-cursor ISI (k > n)

Interference from future symbols — symbols not yet transmitted or detected. Can only be removed by filtering.

Post-cursor ISI (k < n)

Interference from past symbols — already detected! We know these values (or have estimates), so we can subtract them directly.

DFE exploits this split: use a forward filter for pre-cursor ISI and a feedback filter for post-cursor ISI.

Step 3 — DFE Structure

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                   DFE STRUCTURE                      │
  │                                                      │
  │  y[n] ──► [Forward Filter Hff(z)] ──► (+) ──► Decision ──► d̂[n]
  │                                        ↑              │
  │                                        │              │
  │                               [−] ◄── [Feedback Filter Hfb(z)]
  │                                        ↑
  │                                   past decisions d̂[n-1], d̂[n-2]...
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
DFE = Forward Filter (handles pre-cursor ISI) + Feedback Filter (cancels post-cursor ISI)

Forward Filter — Hff(z)

A linear (FIR) filter applied to the received signal y[n]. It handles the pre-cursor ISI — the interference from symbols that haven't been detected yet. This filter is similar to a linear equalizer but shorter, since it only needs to handle future-symbol interference.

Hff(z) = Σ_{i=0}^{Nf−1} cᵢ·z^(−i)Nf taps · cᵢ = forward filter tap weights · processes received signal y[n]

Feedback Filter — Hfb(z)

Uses previously detected symbols d̂[n−1], d̂[n−2], ... to compute and subtract the post-cursor ISI they cause. Since these symbols are already decided, their ISI contribution is known — we subtract it directly, adding no noise!

Hfb(z) = Σ_{k=1}^{Nb} bₖ·z^(−k)Nb taps · bₖ = feedback tap weights · fed by past decision outputs d̂[n−k]

The DFE output before the decision device:

ẑ[n] = Σ cᵢ·y[n−i] − Σ bₖ·d̂[n−k]Forward filter output minus feedback filter output (subtraction removes post-cursor ISI)

Step 4 — Why DFE Has Much Less Noise Enhancement

The key reason: The feedback filter operates on detected symbols (binary or M-ary values like ±1, ±3), NOT on the noisy received signal. Detected symbols have no noise attached to them (assuming correct detection). So the feedback path introduces zero additional noise. Only the forward filter processes the noisy received signal — and it's a shorter, less aggressive filter.

Step 5 — Error Propagation: DFE's Weakness

Error Propagation: DFE assumes past decisions are correct. But if a symbol is detected incorrectly (which happens when SNR is low), the wrong value is fed into the feedback filter. This wrong value creates incorrect ISI cancellation — actually adding new interference — which may cause the next symbol to also be detected incorrectly. This chain reaction is called error propagation. 
Error Propagation Example:

Symbol 5 detected wrong → d̂[5] = +1 (should be −1)
    ↓
Feedback filter uses wrong d̂[5]
    ↓
Incorrect ISI cancellation at symbol 6
    ↓
Symbol 6 also detected wrong!
    ↓
Chain reaction continues...
Error propagation — worst at low SNR where initial errors are frequent

Step 6 — When to Use DFE vs Linear vs MLSE

ConditionBest ChoiceReason
High SNR, moderate delay spreadDFELow error probability → minimal error propagation, much less noise enhancement than ZF
Very high SNR, simple channelZF or MMSENoise is negligible anyway, simple implementation
Low SNRMMSEDFE suffers error propagation; MMSE controls noise without feedback
Short delay spread (small L)MLSEOptimal; complexity manageable for small L
Long delay spreadDFEMLSE complexity exponential in L; DFE scales linearly

Step 7 — DFE Performance Comparison

At high SNR, DFE performance approaches MLSE. At moderate SNR it significantly outperforms linear equalizers. The performance gap vs. MLSE is mainly due to error propagation and the fact that forward filter still causes some noise enhancement (just much less than a full linear equalizer).

Intuitive Example — Why DFE Outperforms Linear EQ

Suppose the channel has strong post-cursor ISI: f[0]=1, f[1]=0.9, f[2]=0.8.

Linear ZF equalizer: Must invert this long channel → needs many taps → the 1/F(z) filter has large high-frequency gain → large noise amplification.

DFE: Forward filter only needs to handle f[0] (current symbol effects). Once d̂[n−1] and d̂[n−2] are decided, the feedback filter subtracts 0.9·d̂[n−1] and 0.8·d̂[n−2] directly — no noise added by these operations. The forward filter is much simpler → much less noise enhancement.

§7.3 / §7.8 Rappaport — Adaptive

Adaptive Equalization

Wireless channels change over time. An equalizer designed for yesterday's channel fails today. Adaptive equalization continuously updates the equalizer coefficients to track the changing channel.

Step 1 — Why Adaptation Is Necessary

In a static channel (like a wired link), you design the equalizer once and it works forever. Wireless channels are never static:

  • User mobility — moving phone changes the multipath geometry
  • Moving scatterers — vehicles, people change reflections
  • Doppler shift — motion changes signal frequency
  • Handover — switching between base stations changes channel entirely
📻 Analogy: Imagine tuning a radio station manually while driving. The signal drifts as you move — you keep turning the dial to stay on frequency. Adaptive equalization is the same idea: the equalizer keeps "retuning" its coefficients as the channel drifts.

Step 2 — The Two Phases of Adaptive Equalization

Phase 1: Training

Transmitter sends a known sequence (pilot/training symbols). Receiver knows the exact transmitted data, so it can compute the error between received and expected signals, and use it to set initial tap weights accurately.

Phase 2: Tracking (Decision-Directed)

After training, no more known pilots. The equalizer uses its own output decisions as if they were true symbols to keep updating tap weights. Works well when BER is low (decisions usually correct).

  TIME →
  |Training period | ← Data transmission (tracking) →|
  |[known symbols] | [user data with decision-directed update]|
  |← tap weights  →| ← tap weights continuously adapted →|
  |   converge     |   track channel variations              |
Training establishes initial weights; tracking keeps them current

Step 3 — The General Adaptive Update Rule

All adaptive algorithms share a common structure. Define:

SymbolMeaning
w[n]Vector of tap weights at time n
y[n]Vector of recent received samples (equalizer input)
ẑ[n] = w[n]ᴴ·y[n]Equalizer output at time n
d[n]Desired output (known pilot or hard decision)
e[n] = d[n] − ẑ[n]Error signal — how wrong the equalizer output is

The tap update rule uses the error signal to adjust weights in the direction that reduces error:

w[n+1] = w[n] + μ · e[n] · y*[n] Generic adaptive update · μ = step size · e[n] = error · y*[n] = conjugate of input vector

Step 4 — LMS Algorithm (Least Mean Squares)

What LMS Does

LMS is the simplest and most widely used adaptive algorithm. It approximates the steepest-descent gradient of the mean squared error using just the instantaneous error (one sample at a time), rather than averaging over many samples.

w[n+1] = w[n] + μ · e[n] · y*[n]LMS update rule · identical form to generic rule — LMS IS the stochastic gradient approximation

LMS Step-by-Step Algorithm

  LMS ALGORITHM — repeat at each time step n:
  ════════════════════════════════════════════
  1. Collect input:   y[n] = [y(n), y(n−1), ..., y(n−N+1)]ᵀ

  2. Compute output:  ẑ[n] = w[n]ᴴ · y[n]

  3. Get desired:     d[n] = known pilot (training)
                           OR hard decision (tracking)

  4. Compute error:   e[n] = d[n] − ẑ[n]

  5. Update weights:  w[n+1] = w[n] + μ · e[n] · y*[n]

  6. Go to step 1 for next sample.
  ════════════════════════════════════════════
Each iteration: O(N) operations — very efficient for real-time implementation

The Step Size μ — The Most Critical Parameter

μ too large

Large updates → equalizer overshoots → oscillates around solution → never converges. Can become unstable and diverge.

μ too small

Tiny updates → converges very slowly → can't track fast channel changes. May not converge before channel changes again.

0 < μ < 2/λ_max Stability condition for LMS · λ_max = largest eigenvalue of input autocorrelation matrix R_yy
Practical rule: μ ≈ 1/(N · P_in) N = number of taps · P_in = average input signal power — a safe, commonly used starting value

LMS Properties

PropertyLMS Value
Computation per updateO(N) — very low
Convergence speedModerate — depends on μ and channel eigenvalue spread
Misadjustment (steady-state excess MSE)≈ μ·N·σ²_n (proportional to step size)
Numerical stabilityGood
Memory requiredO(N)
Used inMost practical systems — GSM, IS-136, LTE

Step 5 — RLS Algorithm (Recursive Least Squares)

RLS minimises the sum of all past squared errors (weighted by a forgetting factor λ), not just the current error. It solves the least-squares problem exactly at every step using a recursive update.

Minimise: Σ_{i=1}^{n} λ^(n−i) · |e[i]|² λ = forgetting factor (0 < λ < 1) · older samples receive exponentially less weight

RLS Update Equations

k[n] = P[n−1]·y[n] / (λ + y[n]ᴴ·P[n−1]·y[n]) Kalman gain vector k[n]
w[n] = w[n−1] + k[n] · e*[n] Tap weight update · e[n] = d[n] − w[n−1]ᴴ·y[n]
P[n] = (1/λ)(P[n−1] − k[n]·y[n]ᴴ·P[n−1]) Inverse autocorrelation matrix update · P[n] = (R_yy[n])^(−1)

RLS vs LMS — Detailed Comparison

PropertyLMSRLS
Computation/updateO(N) — cheapO(N²) — expensive
Convergence speedModerate (depends on μ)Fast — typically N steps
Convergence behaviourDepends on eigenvalue spread of R_yyIndependent of eigenvalue spread
Steady-state MSEHigher (misadjustment from μ)Lower (nearly optimal)

Summary rule of thumb: Use LMS when complexity is the main constraint (most practical systems). Use RLS when the channel changes rapidly and fast convergence is critical (e.g. burst-mode communications, rapidly moving vehicles).

Step 6 — The Forgetting Factor λ in RLS

λ close to 1 (e.g. 0.999)

Long memory — uses many past samples. Good for slowly varying channels. Converges to a very accurate estimate. Slow to track rapid changes.

λ smaller (e.g. 0.95)

Short memory — forgets old samples quickly. Tracks fast channel changes well. But noisier estimates due to fewer effective samples.

Step 7 — Training Sequence Design

Good training sequences have special properties that make channel estimation easy and accurate:

  • Known to both transmitter and receiver — obvious requirement
  • Good autocorrelation — ideally delta-function autocorrelation (like a pseudo-noise sequence) for easy channel identification
  • Constant power — avoids dynamic range issues in estimation
  • Sufficient length — must be longer than the channel impulse response length L
  • Periodic in some systems — allows frequency-domain channel estimation efficiently

In GSM, a 26-symbol training sequence is embedded in the middle of each 148-symbol burst. In LTE, pilot symbols are scattered across time-frequency resource elements for continuous tracking.

Step 8 — Adaptive Equalization in Real Systems

How GSM Uses Adaptive Equalization

GSM uses a burst structure: each transmission burst contains 148 bits, with a 26-bit training sequence in the middle.

GSM Burst Structure:
[3 tail][57 data bits][1 flag][26 training][1 flag][57 data bits][3 tail]
                               ↑
                         Known to receiver
                    Used to estimate channel H
                    Then MLSE (Viterbi) equalizer
                    processes entire burst

The 26-bit Barker-like sequence has ideal autocorrelation properties. The receiver uses it with a least-squares algorithm to estimate the channel impulse response, then runs an MLSE equalizer on the full burst.

In LTE/4G: pilot symbols are scattered in the time-frequency grid. The receiver interpolates between pilots to estimate H at all resource elements, then applies MMSE equalization per subcarrier (1 complex multiplication per subcarrier).

Practice — §7.3/7.8 Adaptive Equalization
  1. An LMS equalizer has N=5 taps, step size μ=0.01, and current weights w=[1, 0, 0, 0, 0]ᵀ. At time n, the received signal vector is y=[0.8, 0.3, −0.2, 0.1, 0.05]ᵀ and the known training symbol is d=+1. (a) Compute the equalizer output ẑ[n]. (b) Compute the error e[n]. (c) Compute the updated weight vector w[n+1].
  2. A mobile user is travelling at 120 km/h at 900 MHz. The coherence time Tc ≈ 9/(16πf_D) where f_D = v·f_c/c. Find f_D and Tc. If the symbol rate is 270 kbps (GSM), how many symbols fit in one coherence time? Does the channel change significantly during one GSM burst (148 symbols)?
  3. Compare LMS and RLS for a channel with 8 taps. If convergence in 20 symbol times is required: (a) What step size μ might achieve this for LMS? (b) What forgetting factor λ might achieve this for RLS? (c) Which is computationally cheaper?

1. (a) ẑ[n] = wᴴ·y = 1×0.8 + 0×0.3 + 0×(−0.2) + 0×0.1 + 0×0.05 = 0.8. (b) e[n] = d − ẑ = 1 − 0.8 = 0.2. (c) w[n+1] = w[n] + μ·e·y* = [1,0,0,0,0] + 0.01×0.2×[0.8,0.3,−0.2,0.1,0.05] = [1,0,0,0,0] + [0.0016, 0.0006, −0.0004, 0.0002, 0.0001] = [1.0016, 0.0006, −0.0004, 0.0002, 0.0001]. The weights moved slightly toward the correct values.

2. f_D = v·f_c/c = (120/3.6)×900×10⁶/(3×10⁸) = 33.33×3×10⁶/10⁸ = 100 Hz. Tc = 9/(16π×100) = 9/5027 ≈ 1.79 ms. At 270 kbps: symbols per coherence time = 270,000 × 0.00179 ≈ 483 symbols. A GSM burst is 148 symbols × (1/270,000) ≈ 0.548 ms. Since 0.548 ms ≪ 1.79 ms, the channel is approximately constant within one burst → a single channel estimate per burst is sufficient!

3. (a) LMS convergence: roughly need μ × λ_max > convergence threshold. For fast convergence in ~N=8 iterations (minimum possible), need larger μ, e.g. μ ≈ 1/(10·P_in). But faster convergence means more misadjustment. There's no single answer — it depends on eigenvalue spread. Typical: μ ≈ 0.05/P_in gives moderate convergence. (b) RLS with λ=0.95 effectively uses about 1/(1−0.95)=20 samples worth of data → converges in ~20 iterations. Tune λ ≈ 1−1/20 = 0.95. (c) LMS: 2N=16 multiply-adds/step. RLS: N²=64 multiply-adds/step. LMS is 4× cheaper per update.
