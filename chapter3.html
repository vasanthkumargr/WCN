Chapter 3 — Introduction

What is Fading? — The Multipath Story

Chapter 2 explained how signal power decreases with distance (path loss and shadowing). Chapter 3 zooms in to the fast, random fluctuations that happen even when you stand still — caused by multiple signal copies arriving from different paths.

Step 1 — What is Fading?

Even when a phone doesn't move, received signal strength can fluctuate rapidly. Sometimes strong, sometimes almost zero — this random variation is called fading. It's caused by multipath propagation.

Step 2 — Multipath Propagation

A transmitted signal reaches the receiver via many different paths:

  📡 Transmitter
       │
       ├─────────────────────────── direct path (LOS)
       │
       ├──→ building → reflected ──→  📱 Receiver
       │
       ├──→ car → scattered ─────────→ 📱 Receiver
       │
       └──→ tree → diffracted ───────→ 📱 Receiver

  Receiver gets: copy1 + copy2 + copy3 + copy4
  Each copy has different: time delay, amplitude, phase
Multipath propagation — receiver gets many delayed copies of the same transmitted signal

Step 3 — Constructive and Destructive Addition

Constructive Addition → Strong signal

Signal copies arrive in phase — they add together. The combined signal is stronger than any individual component.

+1 + 1 = +2 → strong
Destructive Addition → Weak signal

Signal copies arrive out of phase — they cancel each other. The combined signal nearly disappears.

+1 + (−1) = 0 → deep fade

Since the receiver moves (or objects move), the relative phases constantly change → signal randomly fluctuates between strong and weak. This is small-scale fading.

Step 4 — Why Statistical Models Are Needed

In Chapter 2 we used ray-tracing for deterministic channels — we knew exactly where every reflector was. In practice:

  • Objects (buildings, cars, people) move constantly
  • Channel changes in unpredictable ways
  • Exact reflector positions are unknown

So instead of exact calculations, we characterize the channel using probability distributions — describing the random amplitude, phase, and delay of each multipath component.

Step 5 — Delay Spread and Its Effects

  Transmit one pulse:

  |█|  ← transmitted pulse (duration T)

  Receive pulse train:
  |█|   |█|   |█|    ← multiple delayed copies
   t1    t2    t3

  Delay spread Tm = t3 − t1 (last arrival − first arrival)

The impact depends on the ratio of delay spread to symbol duration:

ConditionChannel typeEffect
Tm ≪ T (symbol duration)NarrowbandAll multipath overlaps → amplitude/phase fading only, no time spreading
Tm ≈ T or Tm ≫ TWidebandMultipath components are resolvable → ISI between symbols

Practice — §3 Introduction
  1. A wireless channel has 4 multipath components arriving at t = 0, 50, 150, 300 ns. (a) What is the delay spread Tm? (b) If signal bandwidth = 5 MHz, is this narrowband or wideband? (hint: compare Tm to 1/B)
  2. Explain why two receivers at exactly the same distance from a base station can have very different received power due to small-scale fading, even though their path loss is identical.

1. (a) Tm = 300 − 0 = 300 ns. (b) 1/B = 1/5×10⁶ = 200 ns. Since Tm = 300 ns > 200 ns, the delay spread exceeds the inverse bandwidth → wideband channel → multipath components are resolvable → ISI will occur.

2. Even at the same distance, the specific set of reflectors between each receiver and the base station is different. One receiver might have 3 copies arriving nearly in-phase (constructive → strong signal) while the other has copies arriving out of phase (destructive → weak signal, deep fade). The path-loss model only predicts the average power at that distance; small-scale fading causes large random deviations of ±20–30 dB around this average.

Section 3.1 — Time-Varying Channel Impulse Response

Modelling the Multipath Channel Mathematically

The time-varying impulse response c(τ,t) is the core mathematical tool for describing multipath channels. This section builds it from the transmitted signal all the way to the final compact expression.

Step 1 — Transmitted Signal (Recap)

s(t) = Re{u(t)·e^(j2πfct)} Same as Chapter 2 · u(t) = complex baseband signal with bandwidth Bu · fc = carrier frequency

The signal u(t) contains the actual data. It is upconverted to carrier frequency fc for transmission through the antenna.

Step 2 — Received Signal: Sum of All Multipath Copies

r(t) = Re{ Σₙ αₙ(t)·u(t−τₙ(t))·e^(j(2πfc(t−τₙ(t))+φDn)) } Eq. (3.2) · sum over n = 0 (LOS) to N(t) multipath components · each term = one signal copy

The receiver gets the sum of N(t)+1 copies of the signal, each delayed by a different amount and scaled by a different amplitude.

Step 3 — What Each Parameter Means

ParameterSymbolPhysical meaningChanges because of…
Number of pathsN(t)How many distinct signal paths exist at time tObjects appearing/disappearing
Path amplitudeαₙ(t)Strength of the n-th path — depends on path loss and reflection lossesShadowing, reflection angle changes
Path delayτₙ(t) = rₙ(t)/cHow long the n-th path takes to arriveReceiver/reflector movement changes path length rₙ
Doppler phase shiftφDn(t) = ∫2πfDn dtPhase accumulation from Doppler effectVelocity v and arrival angle θₙ change
Doppler frequencyfDn = v·cosθₙ/λFrequency shift of n-th path due to motionSpeed and direction of motion

Step 4 — Resolvable vs Non-Resolvable Multipath

Two multipath components with delays τ₁ and τ₂ are resolvable if:

|τ₁ − τ₂| ≫ 1/BuTheir delay difference significantly exceeds the inverse signal bandwidth · Bu = signal bandwidth
Resolvable (wideband signals)

Receiver sees separate pulse echoes. Each component is distinguishable. Typical in wideband systems (radar, UWB, CDMA).

|pulse1| |pulse2|

Non-resolvable (narrowband signals)

Components arrive so close in time they merge into one combined signal. Their random phases cause fast fading amplitude variations.

|combined pulse|

🔍
Intuition: Signal bandwidth determines time resolution: resolution ≈ 1/Bu. A 10 MHz signal resolves paths separated by 100 ns. A 10 kHz signal cannot separate paths unless they differ by 100 µs. So wideband systems see many separate echoes; narrowband systems see one fluctuating combined signal.

Step 5 — Simplified Phase Term

Define the combined phase for each path:

φₙ(t) = 2π·fc·τₙ(t) − φDn(t) Combines the delay-induced phase (2πfcτn) and the Doppler phase shift (φDn) into one term

The received signal simplifies to:

r(t) = Re{ Σₙ αₙ(t)·e^(−jφₙ(t))·u(t−τₙ(t))·e^(j2πfct) } Eq. (3.4) — each path multiplies u(t) by a complex scaling αₙe^(−jφₙ) and delays it by τₙ

Step 6 — Channel as a Linear Filter: Convolution Form

r(t) = Re{ [∫ c(τ,t)·u(t−τ) dτ] · e^(j2πfct) } Eq. (3.5) — the received baseband signal = convolution of u(t) with the channel impulse response c(τ,t)
🎚️
Why convolution? The channel acts exactly like a linear filter on the baseband signal u(t). A filter's output = input convolved with the filter's impulse response. Here c(τ,t) is the channel's "filter" — but unlike a fixed filter, it changes with time t.

Step 7 — The Time-Varying Impulse Response c(τ,t)

c(τ,t) = Σₙ αₙ(t)·e^(−jφₙ(t))·δ(τ − τₙ(t)) Eq. (3.6) — sum of delta functions at each multipath delay · each delta weighted by complex amplitude αₙe^(−jφₙ)

  At time t1 (3 paths):       At time t2 (2 paths):
  Power                        Power

c(τ,t) is like a movie of the channel impulse response — each frame is a different time t

Delta Function Role

The delta function δ(τ − τₙ) is zero everywhere except at exactly τ = τₙ. Using the sifting property: ∫δ(τ−τₙ)·u(t−τ) dτ = u(t−τₙ). So each delta function "picks out" one delayed copy of the signal.

Example 3.1 — Conveyor Belt Channel (Textbook)

A wireless LAN near a conveyor belt. LOS path always exists (gain α₀, phase φ₀, delay τ₀). Every T₀ seconds a metal object passes, creating an extra reflected path (gain α₁, phase φ₁, delay τ₁).

When NO metal object (t ≠ nT₀)

Only LOS path. Channel has one spike:

c(τ,t) = α₀·e^(jφ₀)·δ(τ−τ₀)
When metal object present (t = nT₀)

LOS + reflection. Channel has two spikes:

c(τ,t) = α₀e^(jφ₀)δ(τ−τ₀) + α₁e^(jφ₁)δ(τ−τ₁)

This is a simple but concrete illustration of a time-varying channel — the channel literally switches between two different impulse responses periodically.

Step 8 — Why Small Delay Changes Cause Rapid Fading

Phase of the n-th path: φₙ = 2π·fc·τₙ

Example: fc = 1 GHz, τₙ = 50 ns → fc·τₙ = 50 (50 complete cycles!)

A tiny change in path length by just λ/2 = 15 cm changes the delay by 0.5 ns, which changes the phase by 2π·1GHz·0.5ns = π radians (180°). That's the difference between fully constructive and fully destructive interference!

Key insight: Because fc·τₙ ≫ 1, even tiny movements (centimetres) cause phase to cycle through 0°→360°→0°... rapidly. This is why small-scale fading happens over distances of just a few wavelengths — moving 12.5 cm at 2.4 GHz changes your fading completely.

Step 9 — Typical Delay Spread Values

EnvironmentDelay Spread TmWhy
Indoor (office/home)10–1000 nsSmall spaces, fewer reflectors, close walls
Suburban200–2000 nsHouses, trees at medium distances
Urban1–30 µsTall buildings create very long reflection paths
Practice — §3.1
  1. A channel has 3 paths: (α₁=1.0, τ₁=0, φ₁=0), (α₂=0.5, τ₂=100ns, φ₂=π/2), (α₃=0.3, τ₃=250ns, φ₃=π). Write out c(τ,t) assuming the channel is time-invariant.
  2. At fc = 2.4 GHz, a receiver moves 6 cm toward the transmitter. (a) What wavelength λ? (b) How much does the path delay change? (c) What is the phase change? Is the signal now more constructive or destructive compared to before?

1. Time-invariant: c(τ) = 1.0·e^(j0)·δ(τ−0) + 0.5·e^(jπ/2)·δ(τ−100ns) + 0.3·e^(jπ)·δ(τ−250ns) = δ(τ) + 0.5j·δ(τ−100ns) − 0.3·δ(τ−250ns). Three spikes at τ=0, 100ns, 250ns with complex weights 1, 0.5j, −0.3.

2. (a) λ = c/fc = 3×10⁸/2.4×10⁹ = 0.125 m = 12.5 cm. (b) Moving 6 cm toward transmitter reduces path length by 6 cm, so delay decreases by 6×10⁻²/(3×10⁸) = 0.2 ns. (c) Phase change = 2π·fc·Δτ = 2π×2.4×10⁹×0.2×10⁻⁹ = 2π×0.48 = 0.96π ≈ 173°. That's almost 180° — from nearly constructive to nearly destructive (or vice versa). Just 6 cm of movement completely changes the fading condition!

Section 3.3 — Wideband Fading Models

Wideband Fading — When Multipath Spreads the Signal in Time

In narrowband fading, all multipath arrives simultaneously and just changes amplitude. In wideband fading, separate echoes arrive at different times — causing the signal to smear in time and interfere with subsequent symbols.

Step 1 — The Core Difference: Narrowband vs Wideband

Narrowband: Tm ≪ T

All multipath signals arrive roughly at same time. They combine into one signal with random amplitude and phase. No time spreading. Received signal duration ≈ T.

Sent:  |████|
Recv:  |████|  (same duration, random amp)
Wideband: Tm ≈ T or Tm ≫ T

Multipath arrivals are spaced apart in time. Received signal spreads to duration T + Tm. Multiple echoes visible. ISI occurs.

Sent:  |████|
Recv:  |████| |███| |██|  (spread out!)
Critical boundary: When signal bandwidth B ≈ 1/Tm, the narrowband approximation u(t−τₙ) ≈ u(t) breaks down. The received signal becomes a distorted sum of delayed copies rather than a scaled/shifted version of the original.

Step 2 — Inter-Symbol Interference (ISI)

  Transmit symbols:  |Sym1|  |Sym2|  |Sym3|
  Each symbol duration = T

  Wideband channel with Tm > T:
  Sym1 copies:  |Sym1|  |Sym1|  |Sym1|
                        ↑overlap ↑overlap
  Sym2:                 |Sym2|
  Sym3:                        |Sym3|

  Received at time of Sym2: Sym1_echo2 + Sym2 + Sym1_echo3
                                         ↑ ISI contamination!
ISI — previous symbols' echoes arrive during the current symbol's time slot

Step 3 — ISI Mitigation Techniques

TechniqueHow it worksChapter
EqualizationReceiver filter inverts channel distortion in time domainCh. 11
OFDM / MulticarrierSplit data into many slow subcarriers — each has long symbol time Ts ≫ TmCh. 12
Spread SpectrumIntentionally spread signal bandwidth — use rake receiver to combine copiesCh. 13

Step 4 — Statistical Model for Wideband Channels

When many multipath components exist with random phases, by the Central Limit Theorem, c(τ,t) is a complex Gaussian process. Its envelope is Rayleigh-distributed — same as narrowband fading, but now the multipath components are resolvable in the delay dimension τ.

Step 5 — Scattering Function Sc(τ, ρ)

To capture both delay spread and Doppler effects, we take the Fourier transform of c(τ,t) with respect to t:

Sc(τ, ρ) = ∫ c(τ,t)·e^(−j2πρt) dt Eq. (3.49) — Scattering function · τ = multipath delay axis · ρ = Doppler frequency axis

The scattering function shows how signal power is distributed across both delay τ and Doppler ρ. It's a 2D picture of the channel — a typical Sc(τ,ρ) plot looks like a "cloud" of power spread in both dimensions.

Step 6 — Channel Autocorrelation Ac(τ₁, τ₂; Δt)

For random channels we use the statistical autocorrelation:

Ac(τ₁,τ₂; t, t+Δt) = E[c*(τ₁;t)·c(τ₂;t+Δt)] Eq. (3.50) — expected correlation between channel responses at two delays and two times

Step 7 — The WSSUS Channel Model

Wide-Sense Stationary Uncorrelated Scattering (WSSUS)

Most real wireless channels satisfy two properties:

WSS (Wide-Sense Stationary): Channel statistics don't change with absolute time t — only the time difference Δt matters. So Ac(τ₁,τ₂; t, t+Δt) = Ac(τ₁,τ₂; Δt).

US (Uncorrelated Scattering): Multipath components from different delays are independent of each other (caused by different physical reflectors):

E[c*(τ₁;t)·c(τ₂;t+Δt)] = Ac(τ;Δt)·δ(τ₁−τ₂)Eq. (3.52) — τ₁ ≠ τ₂ components are uncorrelated · simplifies to a function of single delay τ and Δt

The WSSUS model, introduced by Bello (1963), is the standard model for practical wireless channels. It means: different delay taps evolve independently, and the statistics of each tap don't drift with time.

Practice — §3.3 Wideband
  1. A channel has delay spread Tm = 5 µs. A system uses symbol duration Ts = 0.5 µs. (a) Is this narrowband or wideband? (b) Will there be ISI? (c) Approximately how many symbol periods does the ISI span?
  2. Explain why OFDM (multicarrier) avoids ISI even in a wideband channel. What property of OFDM's subcarrier symbols makes them essentially narrowband?
  3. Why does the WSSUS assumption "uncorrelated scattering" make physical sense? What would it mean if two different delay components WERE correlated?

1. (a) Tm/Ts = 5/0.5 = 10 ≫ 1 → definitely wideband. (b) Yes, severe ISI. (c) The ISI spans approximately Tm/Ts = 10 symbol periods — the echo of symbol k contaminates symbols k+1, k+2, ..., k+10.

2. OFDM divides the data into N parallel substreams. Each subcarrier has symbol duration TN = N × Ts — much longer. If N is chosen so TN ≫ Tm, each subcarrier's symbol time exceeds the delay spread → essentially narrowband on each subcarrier → no ISI. The cyclic prefix (Chapter 12) absorbs the remaining ISI at subcarrier boundaries.

3. US makes sense because multipath components at different delays τ₁ ≠ τ₂ come from physically distinct reflectors — a building on the left vs. a car on the right. Their amplitudes and phases depend on completely different physical objects, so they should be statistically independent. If they were correlated, it would mean information about one reflector tells you about another distant reflector — physically unreasonable for independent scattering objects.

Section 3.3.1 — Power Delay Profile

Power Delay Profile — How Signal Power Spreads Over Delay

The Power Delay Profile (PDP) is the key measurement of a wideband channel. It shows how much power arrives at each delay τ, and from it we compute the delay spread that determines ISI severity.

Step 1 — What is the Power Delay Profile?

Ac(τ) ≜ Ac(τ, Δt=0) PDP = the autocorrelation at zero time difference · = average power associated with multipath delay τ 
  Power
    ↑
    |*
    | *
    |  **
    |    ***
    |       ****
    |           *****
    +──────────────────→ delay τ
    0                  Tm

  Each point on the curve shows how much average power arrives at that delay.
  Strong early arrivals (LOS + strong reflections), weaker late arrivals.
Power Delay Profile — power-weighted distribution of multipath delays

Step 2 — Mean Delay Spread

μTm = ∫₀^∞ τ·Ac(τ) dτ / ∫₀^∞ Ac(τ) dτ Eq. (3.54) — power-weighted average delay · like the "centre of mass" of the PDP · not always a good measure of spread

Step 3 — RMS Delay Spread

σTm = √( ∫₀^∞ (τ−μTm)²·Ac(τ) dτ / ∫₀^∞ Ac(τ) dτ ) Eq. (3.55) — standard deviation of the delay distribution · measures how spread out the multipath delays are around the mean
⚖️
Why σTm is preferred over μTm: Mean delay spread μTm can be large even when all paths arrive at roughly the same time (e.g., two paths at 9 µs and 11 µs give μ=10 µs but σ=1 µs — the actual spreading is small). σTm correctly captures the variation around the mean. Engineers typically synchronise to the mean delay, so σTm measures the residual time spreading relative to that synchronisation point.

Step 4 — Power Weighting: Why Weak Paths Don't Matter Much

The PDP acts like a probability distribution: pTm(τ) = Ac(τ) / ∫Ac(τ)dτ. Weak multipath components (low Ac(τ)) contribute little to delay spread. Components below the noise floor are effectively ignored. This is physically correct — you can't be "interfered with" by a signal you can't receive.

Step 5 — ISI Condition Based on σTm

ConditionISI levelPractical rule
Ts > 10·σTmNegligible ISI ✓Safe to transmit at this symbol rate
Ts ≈ σTmModerate ISI ⚠May need equalization
Ts < σTm/10Severe ISI ✗Must use equalization, OFDM, or spread spectrum

Engineers typically assume Ac(τ) ≈ 0 for τ > 3·σTm, so the "effective" channel length ≈ 3·σTm.

Example 3.4 — Exponential PDP (Textbook)

Model: Ac(τ) = (1/T̄m)·e^(−τ/T̄m) for τ ≥ 0 (exponential decay — common model for many real channels)

Mean delay spread: μTm = ∫₀^∞ τ·(1/T̄m)·e^(−τ/T̄m) dτ = T̄m (standard result for exponential distribution)

RMS delay spread: σTm = √(∫₀^∞ (τ−T̄m)²·(1/T̄m)·e^(−τ/T̄m) dτ) = √(2T̄m² − T̄m²) = T̄m

Result: For exponential PDP: μTm = σTm = T̄m. Mean and RMS delay spread are equal — a useful property of the exponential model.
Example 3.5 — Maximum Symbol Rate (Textbook)

Given: Ac(τ) = e^(−τ/0.00001) for 0 ≤ τ ≤ 20 µs (exponential PDP, truncated).

Mean delay spread: μTm = 6.87 µs (numerical integration)

RMS delay spread: σTm = 5.25 µs

For negligible ISI: Ts > 10·σTm = 52.5 µs

Maximum symbol rate: Rs = 1/Ts = 1/52.5×10⁻⁶ = 19.04 kSymbols/s

This is extremely low! Voice needs ~32 kbps, data needs Mbps. A channel with 5 µs RMS delay spread limits unequalized data to ~19 kbps. This is why equalization, OFDM, and spread spectrum are essential for modern wireless systems.
Practice — §3.3.1 Power Delay Profile
  1. A channel has PDP: Ac(τ) = 1 for 0 ≤ τ ≤ 4 µs, 0 otherwise (uniform PDP). Find (a) μTm, (b) σTm, (c) the maximum symbol rate for negligible ISI.
  2. Two channels: Channel A has σTm = 1 µs; Channel B has σTm = 10 µs. Both use the same modulation. Which requires more transmit power for equalization, and why?
  3. A system must support voice (32 kbps, BPSK → 32 ksymbols/s). What is the maximum RMS delay spread the channel can have for negligible ISI without equalization?

1. For uniform Ac(τ) = 1 over [0, 4µs]: (a) μTm = ∫₀^4 τ dτ / ∫₀^4 dτ = [τ²/2]₀^4 / 4 = 8/4 = 2 µs. (b) σ²Tm = ∫₀^4 (τ−2)² dτ / 4 = [(τ−2)³/3]₋₂^2 / 4 = (8/3+8/3)/4 = (16/3)/4 = 4/3. σTm = √(4/3) ≈ 1.155 µs. (c) Ts > 10×1.155 µs = 11.55 µs. Rs = 1/11.55µs ≈ 86.6 kSymbols/s.

2. Channel B (σTm = 10 µs) requires more transmit power because: (1) ISI is more severe — more symbols are contaminated by each previous symbol's echoes; (2) The equalizer must have more taps (≈ Tm/Ts more taps for Channel B); (3) The noise enhancement in the equalizer is larger for channels with deeper spectral nulls, which are more likely with greater delay spread. Channel A with σTm = 1 µs is 10× more benign.

3. Rs = 32 kSymbols/s → Ts = 1/32000 = 31.25 µs. For negligible ISI: Ts > 10·σTm → σTm < Ts/10 = 31.25/10 = 3.125 µs. The channel must have σTm < 3.125 µs for unequalized voice communication. Urban channels (1–30 µs) often exceed this, which is why GSM uses an equalizer.

Section 3.3.3 — Doppler Spectrum & Coherence Time

Doppler Spectrum & Coherence Time — How Fast the Channel Changes

The delay spread tells us about spatial spreading. Motion introduces temporal spreading — the channel varies in time. Doppler spread and coherence time characterise how fast this variation is.

Step 1 — Why the Channel Changes With Time

When any object moves (transmitter, receiver, or scatterers), the multipath geometry changes → path lengths rₙ(t) change → delays τₙ(t) change → phases φₙ(t) = 2πfc·τₙ(t) change. The result: the constructive/destructive combination changes, so received signal strength varies over time.

🚑 Doppler effect: Moving toward a transmitter → wavefronts arrive more frequently → received frequency slightly higher. Moving away → lower. Each multipath component arriving from a different angle θ has a different Doppler shift: fDn = v·cosθₙ/λ. Since all paths have different arrival angles, the received signal contains a spread of Doppler frequencies.

Step 2 — Channel Autocorrelation in Time: AC(Δt)

How similar is the channel at two different times separated by Δt?

AC(Δt) = E[c*(τ; t)·c(τ; t+Δt)] Autocorrelation of channel at zero delay offset · measures how correlated the channel is at times t and t+Δt 
  |AC(Δt)|
    ↑
  1 |*****
    |    ****
    |       ***
    |          **
    |            **
  0 |______________→ Δt
                  Tc (coherence time)

  When AC(Tc) ≈ 0: channel has "forgotten" its earlier state → independent
Channel autocorrelation decreases as time separation Δt increases

Step 3 — Doppler Power Spectrum SC(ρ)

SC(ρ) = ∫ AC(Δt)·e^(−j2πρΔt) d(Δt) Eq. (3.62) — Fourier transform of AC(Δt) · ρ = Doppler frequency · gives power spectral density of received signal vs Doppler

The Doppler power spectrum shows how received signal power is distributed across Doppler frequencies. It is zero outside ±BD (Doppler spread), where BD = v/λ for the maximum Doppler shift.

Step 4 — Doppler Spread BD

BD = maximum Doppler frequency = v/λ For stationary reflectors and moving receiver at velocity v · λ = c/fc · BD is the range of Doppler frequencies in SC(ρ)

Step 5 — Coherence Time Tc

Tc ≈ 1/BD Coherence time = duration over which channel remains approximately constant · inverse of Doppler spread
Large Doppler spread BD
↓ Small Tc
Channel changes fast → hard to track
Small Doppler spread BD
↑ Large Tc
Channel changes slowly → easy to track

If channel samples are taken more than Tc seconds apart, they are approximately uncorrelated (and since c(τ,t) is Gaussian, uncorrelated → independent). Samples within Tc are correlated — the channel looks similar.

Example 3.7 — Independent Sample Spacing (Textbook)

Given: Doppler spread BD = 80 Hz

Coherence time: Tc ≈ 1/BD = 1/80 = 0.0125 s = 12.5 ms

Interpretation: Samples of the received signal spaced 12.5 ms apart are approximately uncorrelated — treating them as independent is a good approximation.

Practical implication: Channel estimation must be repeated at least every 12.5 ms. In GSM (270 kbps), 12.5 ms = 3375 symbols — the 26-symbol training sequence per burst (every ~4.6 ms) is more than adequate to track the channel.

Practice — §3.3.3 Doppler & Coherence Time
  1. A mobile user travels at 90 km/h with fc = 1.8 GHz. Find (a) λ, (b) max Doppler shift fD, (c) coherence time Tc. If the channel estimation period is 5 ms, is the estimate still valid?
  2. A satellite channel has coherence time Tc = 1 hour and delay spread σTm = 0.1 ns. (a) What is the Doppler spread? (b) What is the coherence bandwidth? (c) Is this channel fast-fading or slow-fading?
  3. Fill in the table for a mobile communication system: B = 5 MHz, fc = 2 GHz, v = 60 km/h, σTm = 2 µs. Find: coherence bandwidth, Doppler spread, coherence time. Is B > Bc (frequency selective)? Is Ts < Tc (slow fading)?

1. (a) λ = 3×10⁸/1.8×10⁹ = 0.167 m. (b) v = 90/3.6 = 25 m/s. fD = v/λ = 25/0.167 = 150 Hz. (c) Tc ≈ 1/BD = 1/150 ≈ 6.67 ms. Estimation period = 5 ms < Tc = 6.67 ms → YES, the estimate is still approximately valid (channel hasn't changed significantly in 5 ms).

2. (a) BD ≈ 1/Tc = 1/3600 ≈ 0.000278 Hz — essentially zero Doppler. (b) Bc ≈ 1/σTm = 1/0.1×10⁻⁹ = 10 GHz — enormous coherence bandwidth! (c) Satellite channels are extremely slow-fading (Tc = 1 hour) with very wide coherence bandwidth. The satellite moves slowly relative to wavelength, and the only significant path is direct LOS. Almost ideal AWGN channel — no fading concern.

3. Bc ≈ 1/σTm = 1/2×10⁻⁶ = 500 kHz. B = 5 MHz > Bc = 500 kHz → YES, frequency selective fading. Doppler: v=60/3.6=16.67 m/s, λ=0.15m. BD = 16.67/0.15 = 111 Hz. Tc ≈ 1/111 = 9 ms. Ts = 1/B = 0.2 µs ≪ Tc = 9 ms → slow fading (channel essentially constant during one symbol). Summary: frequency-selective slow fading — common in urban LTE.
