Chapter 2 — Introduction

Why Wireless Is Hard — Three Propagation Effects

Before any formula, understand the physical story. A wireless signal leaves a transmitter and must arrive at a receiver with enough strength to be decoded. Three distinct physical effects each attack it differently.

Step 1 — The Core Problem

The wireless radio channel is not like a wire. A wire delivers almost exactly what you put in. The air between a phone and a tower introduces:

  • Noise — random electrical disturbances corrupt bits
  • Interference — other devices' signals mix with yours
  • Channel impairments — the physical world weakens and distorts the signal
  • Time variation — user moves, trucks drive by, weather changes → channel constantly shifts in unpredictable ways

Step 3 — Propagation Models This Chapter Covers

  • Free-space model — simplest: no obstacles, straight line, open space
  • Ray-tracing models — physics-based, tracks reflections (two-ray model)
  • Empirical models — curve-fit to real measurements for specific environments
  • Simplified path-loss model — one equation with a single exponent γ, used for system design
  • Log-normal shadowing model — statistical model for random blockage

Multipath (small-scale fading) is covered in Chapter 3. This chapter focuses on large-scale effects.

Section 2.1 — Radio Wave Propagation

From Maxwell to 5G — The Story of Radio Waves

Understanding where radio waves came from helps build intuition for how they behave.

The Timeline

YearPersonAchievement
1864James Clerk MaxwellMathematically predicted electromagnetic waves must exist — gave us Maxwell's equations
1887Heinrich HertzExperimentally proved radio waves exist — the unit "Hz" is named after him. He thought they had no practical use!
1894Oliver LodgeBuilt first wireless communication system — but only 150 m range
1897Guglielmo MarconiTransmitted radio 18 miles (Isle of Wight to a tugboat)
1901Guglielmo MarconiCrossed the Atlantic Ocean wirelessly — proved global wireless was possible
1906Reginald FessendenFirst voice + music transmission using Amplitude Modulation (AM)
🎵
Why modulation was the key invention: Hertz was right that low-frequency signals (like voice) propagate poorly. Fessenden's solution: shift the voice signal to a high-frequency carrier wave — that's modulation. Every wireless system today (WiFi, 4G, 5G, radio) does exactly this.

How Radio Waves Interact with the Environment

When electromagnetic waves travel in real environments, objects cause three physical effects:

Reflection

Signal bounces off smooth surfaces (buildings, ground, water). Like light reflecting from a mirror.

Scattering

Signal hits rough or small surfaces and spreads in many directions. Trees, rough walls, lamp posts.

Diffraction

Signal bends around sharp edges and corners. Allows signal to reach areas not in direct line-of-sight.

Absorption

Some materials absorb signal energy (wet ground, foliage, human bodies). Strong absorption = signal blocked.

These four effects combined create the complex multipath environment that makes wireless channels so challenging to model.

Why Perfect Maxwell Equation Solutions Are Impossible

Theoretically, solving Maxwell's coupled differential equations with correct boundary conditions gives the exact received signal. Practically: you'd need to know the exact shape, size, material, and dielectric constant of every building, tree, and vehicle — and re-solve whenever anything moves. Instead, engineers use three levels of approximation:

  • Ray tracing: treat wavefronts as particles, track reflections/refractions — good when environment is known and reflections are few
  • Empirical models: go measure signal power in real environments, fit formulas to the data — practical but environment-specific
  • Statistical models: describe the channel with probability distributions — used when environment is unknown or highly variable
Section 2.2 — Transmit and Receive Signal Models

Mathematical Signal Models

Before computing path loss, we need a mathematical description of what is transmitted and what is received. This section builds that foundation.

Step 1 — Operating Frequency Range

BandFrequencySystemsWhy suitable
UHF0.3–3 GHz4G LTE, GSM, WiFi 2.4 GHzGood propagation, practical antenna size
SHF3–30 GHz5G, WiFi 5 GHz, mmWaveMore bandwidth available, smaller antennas

Antenna size ∝ wavelength = c/f. At 1 GHz: λ = 30 cm. At 3 GHz: λ = 10 cm. Compact enough for phones and routers.

Step 2 — Transmitted Signal Model

s(t) = Re{u(t)·e^(j2πfct)} Eq. (2.1) · fc = carrier frequency · u(t) = complex baseband (envelope) signal

Expanding using Euler's formula:

s(t) = sI(t)·cos(2πfct) − sQ(t)·sin(2πfct) sI(t) = Re{u(t)} = in-phase component · sQ(t) = Im{u(t)} = quadrature component

I and Q Components — Why Two?

Using two orthogonal carriers (cos and sin) allows sending twice the information in the same bandwidth. This is the foundation of QAM, QPSK, and all modern digital modulation:

  I-channel: data bits → sI(t) → modulates cos(2πfct)
  Q-channel: data bits → sQ(t) → modulates sin(2πfct)
  Combined signal: s(t) = sI(t)cos(·) - sQ(t)sin(·)

  cos and sin are orthogonal → no interference between I and Q
  → Two independent data streams in same bandwidth!

Complex Envelope u(t)

u(t) = sI(t) + j·sQ(t) is called the complex envelope or equivalent lowpass signal. It contains all the information about the signal (amplitude, phase, data) but without the high carrier frequency. Working with u(t) is much simpler mathematically than s(t).

Transmit power: Pt = Pu/2    where Pu = power of u(t)

Step 3 — Received Signal Model

r(t) = Re{v(t)·e^(j2πfct)} + n(t) Eq. (2.2) · v(t) = channel-modified envelope · n(t) = additive noise from channel

For a time-invariant channel: v(t) = u(t) * c(t) (convolution with channel impulse response c(t)). The channel acts like a filter on the baseband signal.

The SNR = power of signal term / power of noise n(t). In this chapter noise is neglected — focus is on propagation, which determines how v(t) relates to u(t) through path loss and shadowing.

Step 4 — Doppler Shift

fD = v·cosθ / λ Eq. (2.3) · v = receiver velocity · θ = signal arrival angle relative to direction of motion · λ = c/fc · positive if moving toward transmitter
🚑
Intuition: Exactly like the ambulance siren — higher pitch when approaching, lower when receding. When moving toward the transmitter, each successive wavecrest travels slightly less distance → arrives slightly earlier → frequency appears higher. Typical value: at 75 km/h and 1 GHz, fD ≈ 69 Hz — tiny compared to GHz, so ignored in this chapter's models.

Phase change from movement: Δφ = 2π·v·Δt·cosθ / λ. Doppler = rate of phase change / 2π.

Step 5 — Path Loss Definitions

PL = Pt / Pr     (linear) Eq. (2.4) — ratio of transmitted to received power (always ≥ 1 since channel only attenuates)
PL_dB = 10·log₁₀(Pt/Pr)   dB Eq. (2.5) — dB path loss · always non-negative · Path GAIN = −PL_dB (always negative)
Quick Example — Reading Path Loss

Pt = 100 W, Pr = 0.01 W: PL = 100/0.01 = 10,000. PL_dB = 10·log₁₀(10,000) = 40 dB. Path gain = −40 dB. Physically: the signal lost 99.99% of its power.

Practice — §2.2
  1. A transmitter outputs 2 W. The receiver gets 0.002 W. Find (a) linear path loss, (b) path loss in dB, (c) path gain in dB.
  2. A mobile user moves at 60 km/h toward a base station. Carrier frequency fc = 2.4 GHz. Find the Doppler shift fD.

1. (a) PL = 2/0.002 = 1000. (b) PL_dB = 10·log₁₀(1000) = 30 dB. (c) PG = −30 dB.

2. v = 60 km/h = 60/3.6 = 16.67 m/s. λ = c/fc = 3×10⁸/2.4×10⁹ = 0.125 m. θ = 0 (moving directly toward). fD = 16.67×cos(0)/0.125 = 16.67/0.125 = 133 Hz.

Section 2.3 — Free-Space Path Loss

Free-Space Path Loss — The Simplest Model

No obstacles, no reflections, perfect open space, direct line of sight. The simplest possible wireless scenario — and still the foundation for all other models.

Step 1 — What "Free Space" Means

Free-space assumes: no obstacles, no reflections, just empty space between transmitter and receiver with a straight-line (Line-of-Sight, LOS) path. Examples: satellite links, open rural terrain, microwave towers.

Step 2 — Why Signal Weakens Even in Free Space

🔦 Light bulb analogy: A light bulb radiates in all directions. Close to the bulb it's bright; far away it's dim. This is because the same total power spreads over the surface of a sphere of area 4πd². As d doubles, area quadruples, so power per unit area (intensity) quarters. Radio waves do exactly the same.

Step 3 — Power Ratio Formula

Pr/Pt = (√Gl · λ / 4πd)² Eq. (2.7) · Gl = combined transmit+receive antenna gain · λ = c/fc = signal wavelength · d = distance

The Two Key Relationships

Inverse-Square Law: Pr ∝ 1/d²

Doubling distance → power drops 4×. Tripling distance → power drops 9×.

DistanceRelative power
dP
2dP/4 (−6 dB)
10dP/100 (−20 dB)
Frequency effect: Pr ∝ λ² ∝ 1/fc²

Higher frequency = smaller wavelength = less received power. Doubling fc → Pr drops 4×. This is why 5G mmWave needs directional antennas or more transmit power.

Step 4 — Path Loss in dB (Link Budget Form)

Pr_dBm = Pt_dBm + 10·log₁₀(Gl) + 20·log₁₀(λ) − 20·log₁₀(4π) − 20·log₁₀(d) Eq. (2.8) · Each term is simply the dB version of the corresponding factor in the power ratio
PL_dB = −10·log₁₀(Gl·λ²/(4πd)²) Eq. (2.9) · Free-space path loss in dB · always positive · PG = −PL = 10·log₁₀(Gl·λ²/(4πd)²)
Example 2.1 — Indoor LAN Transmit Power (Textbook)

Given: fc = 900 MHz, cell radius d = 100 m, omnidirectional antennas (Gl = 1), minimum Pr = 10 µW.

Find required Pt: Invert Eq. (2.7): Pt = Pr × (4πd/λ)²/Gl

λ = c/fc = 3×10⁸/900×10⁶ = 0.333 m

Pt = 10×10⁻⁶ × (4π×100/0.333)² / 1 = 10×10⁻⁶ × (3770.5)² = 1.45 W

If fc = 5 GHz: λ = 0.06 m (much smaller). Same formula gives Pt = 10×10⁻⁶ × (4π×100/0.06)² = 43.9 kW — 30,000× more power needed!

Key insight: Higher frequency → much higher transmit power needed for same coverage at same distance. This is why 5G millimetre-wave systems use highly directional (beamforming) antennas to compensate.
Practice — §2.3 Free-Space
  1. A satellite link operates at fc = 12 GHz, distance d = 36,000 km, Gl = 10⁶ (highly directional dishes). Find Pr/Pt in dB.
  2. An outdoor WiFi hotspot at 2.4 GHz with Pt = 0.1 W, Gl = 1, d = 50 m. Find Pr in mW under free-space model.
  3. If path loss at 100 m is 60 dB at 1 GHz, what is the path loss at 100 m at 2 GHz? (Assume same Gl, free-space model.)

1. λ = 3×10⁸/12×10⁹ = 0.025 m. d = 36,000×10³ m. Pr/Pt = Gl·λ²/(4πd)² = 10⁶ × 0.025²/(4π×3.6×10⁷)² = 625/(5.109×10¹⁷) = 1.22×10⁻¹⁵ = −119.1 dB. The dish antennas recover 60 dB of this loss.

2. λ = 3×10⁸/2.4×10⁹ = 0.125 m. Pr = Pt × (λ/(4πd))² = 0.1 × (0.125/(4π×50))² = 0.1 × (3.98×10⁻⁴)² = 0.1 × 1.58×10⁻⁷ = 1.58×10⁻⁸ W = 0.0158 µW.

3. Free-space path loss PL_dB = 20·log(4πd/λ) = 20·log(4πd·fc/c). Doubling fc adds 20·log(2) = 6 dB. New path loss = 60 + 6 = 66 dB.

Section 2.4.1 — Two-Ray Model

Two-Ray Model — Adding the Ground Reflection

In most outdoor environments, the signal doesn't just travel through the air directly. It also reflects off the ground. Adding this one reflection changes the power law from d⁻² to d⁻⁴ at large distances.

Step 1 — Two Signal Paths

  Transmitter (height ht)                 Receiver (height hr)
       📡                                       📱
        \                                      /
         \  LOS (direct path, length l)       /
          \────────────────────────────────  /
           \                                /
            \    reflected path            /
             \   (length x + x')          /
              \_________________________  /
                       Ground
Two signal paths: direct LOS of length l, and ground reflection of length x+x'

The receiver gets both signals simultaneously. Their combination depends on the phase difference between them — sometimes they add (strong signal), sometimes they cancel (weak signal).

Step 2 — Phase Difference

φ = 2π(x + x' − l) / λ Phase difference between reflected and direct signal · determines whether they add or cancel

For large distance d (d ≫ ht + hr), using Taylor series approximation:

φ ≈ 4π·ht·hr / (λ·d) Eq. (2.14) · Decreases as d increases · when φ = π → signals fully cancel → critical distance dc

Step 3 — Ground Reflection Coefficient R

At large distances, θ → 0 and R → −1. The reflected signal arrives with opposite sign — it tends to cancel the direct signal. This is why the two-ray model gives much faster power decay than free space.

Step 4 — Power Law at Large Distance

Pr ≈ Pt · Gl · (ht·hr)² / d⁴ Eq. (2.17) · valid for d ≫ dc · d⁻⁴ law instead of d⁻² · note λ disappears!
Pr_dBm = Pt_dBm + 10·log₁₀(Gl) + 20·log₁₀(ht·hr) − 40·log₁₀(d) Eq. (2.18) · −40·log₁₀(d) term ↔ d⁻⁴ power law
Comparison: Free-space: −20 dB/decade (power ∝ d⁻²). Two-ray large d: −40 dB/decade (power ∝ d⁻⁴). At large distances, the ground reflection makes the channel twice as lossy (in dB/decade terms) as free space. Also note λ disappears — the received power becomes frequency-independent!

Step 5 — Critical Distance dc

dc = 4·ht·hr / λ Eq. derived from φ=π · The distance where d⁻² transitions to d⁻⁴ · Below dc: power varies (multipath fading) · Above dc: rapid d⁻⁴ decay

Step 6 — Three Power Regions

  Power (dB)
    |
    |──── Region 1: d < ht ────|── Region 2: ht to dc ──|── Region 3: d > dc ──
    |   Constructive add.      | Maxima and minima       | d⁻⁴ decay
    |   Slow increase          | (multipath fading)      | −40 dB/decade
    |   ∝ 1/(d²+ht²)          |  ←small-scale fading→  |
    |                                                     |
    +─────────────────────────────────────────────────── log(d)
Three distinct regions in the two-ray power vs distance curve
Example 2.2 — Critical Distance (Textbook)

Given: fc = 2 GHz → λ = 3×10⁸/2×10⁹ = 0.15 m

Urban microcell: ht = 10 m, hr = 3 m. dc = 4×10×3/0.15 = 120/0.15 = 800 m

Indoor: ht = 3 m, hr = 2 m. dc = 4×3×2/0.15 = 24/0.15 = 160 m

Practical insight: Urban cells today are ~100 m (much smaller than 800 m) because small cells increase capacity and reduce power. Within 100 m (below dc), power falls off as d⁻² — close to free-space. The d⁻⁴ law only kicks in beyond dc, which provides excellent interference isolation from neighbouring cells.
Practice — §2.4.1 Two-Ray
  1. A rural road has ht = 20 m (transmitter), hr = 1.5 m (car antenna), fc = 900 MHz. Find dc. At d = 5 km (much greater than dc), by what additional factor does the two-ray model predict lower power than free-space?
  2. Two receivers are both 500 m from the base station. One has direct LOS; the other has a reflected path with x+x'−l = 0.2 m at fc = 900 MHz. What is the phase difference φ? Do the two signals add or cancel?
  3. At d = dc, the two-ray model transitions from d⁻² to d⁻⁴. What is the path loss in dB at dc and at 10·dc using the d⁻⁴ formula? (Use ht=10m, hr=3m, fc=900 MHz, Gl=1, Pt=1W.)

1. λ = 3×10⁸/900×10⁶ = 0.333 m. dc = 4×20×1.5/0.333 = 120/0.333 = 360 m. At d=5 km >> dc: two-ray power ∝ d⁻⁴ vs free-space d⁻². Additional loss factor = d² (since d⁻⁴/d⁻² = d⁻²). At d = 5000 m: additional loss = (5000)² / (constant). More precisely: free-space Pr ∝ 1/d², two-ray Pr ∝ (hthr)²/d⁴. Ratio = (hthr)²/d² = (20×1.5)²/5000² = 900/25,000,000 ≈ 3.6×10⁻⁵ = −44.4 dB lower than free-space at same d.

2. φ = 2π(x+x'−l)/λ = 2π×0.2/0.333 = 2π×0.6 = 3.77 rad ≈ 216°. Since 180° < 216° < 360°, the signals are roughly out of phase (close to destructive). At exactly φ=π (180°): complete cancellation. At φ=216°: partial cancellation — received power is reduced.

3. dc = 4×10×3/0.333 = 360 m. At dc=360m using d⁻⁴: Pr = Pt×Gl×(ht×hr)²/d⁴ = 1×1×(30)²/(360)⁴ = 900/(1.68×10¹⁰) = 5.36×10⁻⁸ W = −42.7 dBm. At 10dc=3600m: Pr drops by additional 40log(10) = 40 dB → −82.7 dBm.

Section 2.6 — Simplified Path-Loss Model

Simplified Path-Loss Model — One Equation for All Environments

Real environments are too complex for exact models. Engineers use a simple one-parameter formula that captures the essential distance dependence, calibrated to each environment type.

Step 1 — The Universal Formula

Pr = Pt · K · (d0/d)^γ Eq. (2.39) · K = constant (antennas + average attenuation) · d0 = reference distance · γ = path-loss exponent
Pr_dBm = Pt_dBm + K_dB − 10γ·log₁₀(d/d0) Eq. (2.40) — log-domain form · straight line on dB vs log(d) plot · slope = −10γ dB/decade

Step 2 — The Three Parameters

d0Reference distance — model valid for d > d01–10 m indoors, 10–100 m outdoorsChosen by designer

Why factories have low γ (≈2)? Metal walls and equipment create many reflections — like a mirror box. Signals bounce around efficiently and actually propagate more like free space or better. In contrast, dense urban canyons have γ up to 6.5 because buildings absorb and scatter signals from all directions.

Practice — §2.6 Simplified Model
  1. An urban macrocell system has K_dB = −28 dB, d0 = 100 m, γ = 4.0, Pt = 2 W = 33 dBm. Find Pr in dBm at d = 1 km and d = 5 km.
  2. Two propagation environments: (A) γ = 2.5 and (B) γ = 4.5, both with same K and d0. At d = 100·d0, what is the path loss difference between A and B in dB?
  3. From Example 2.3 measurements: does the actual measurement at d = 100 m (−110 dB) match the model (γ = 3.71)? Compute the model prediction at d = 100 m and the error. What causes this error?

1. At d = 1000 m: Pr = 33 − 28 − 40·log₁₀(1000/100) = 33 − 28 − 40 = −35 dBm. At d = 5000 m: Pr = 33 − 28 − 40·log₁₀(5000/100) = 33 − 28 − 40·log₁₀(50) = 5 − 40×1.699 = 5 − 67.96 = −62.96 dBm.

3. Model at d = 100 m: −31.54 − 10×3.71×log₁₀(100) = −31.54 − 37.1×2 = −31.54 − 74.2 = −105.74 dB. Measured = −110 dB. Error = −110 − (−105.74) = −4.26 dB. This error is caused by shadow fading — a random building or obstacle reduced the signal by an extra 4.26 dB at this location compared to the average model prediction.

Section 2.7 — Shadow Fading

Shadow Fading — Random Signal Blockage

Even if two receivers are at exactly the same distance from a transmitter, their received power can differ significantly. This random variation is shadow fading — caused by unpredictable obstacles in the signal path.

Step 1 — Why Shadow Fading Exists

The simplified path-loss model gives the average power at distance d. But in reality, the received power at a given distance is random because:

  • Buildings of different sizes and materials randomly block the path
  • Trees, vehicles, terrain features create varying amounts of attenuation
  • Reflecting surfaces change → indirect paths strengthen or weaken

These obstacles are unknown and change with location, so a statistical model is needed.

Step 2 — Log-Normal Distribution

Define ψ = Pt/Pr (path loss ratio). Empirical measurements consistently show that ψ follows a log-normal distribution — meaning ψ_dB = 10·log₁₀(ψ) follows a Gaussian (normal) distribution:

ψ_dB ~ N(μ_ψdB, σ_ψdB²) Eq. (2.46) · μ_ψdB = mean path loss in dB · σ_ψdB = standard deviation in dB · Gaussian distribution in dB scale

Step 3 — Physical Justification (Why Log-Normal?)

Central Limit Theorem Argument

Attenuation through one object of depth d: s(d) = e^(−αd)

Through many random objects with total depth dt = Σdᵢ: s(dt) = e^(−α·dt)

By the Central Limit Theorem, the sum of many independent random depths dt is approximately Gaussian. Therefore log s(dt) = −α·dt is Gaussian → s(dt) is log-normal → path loss ψ is log-normal.

In simple words: Each object multiplies the signal attenuation. Many multiplied factors = product of random variables. Log of a product = sum of logs = sum of random variables → Gaussian by CLT. This is why log-normal appears so consistently in wireless measurements.

Step 4 — The Two Parameters

ParameterSymbolMeaningTypical values
Mean path loss (dB)μ_ψdBAverage attenuation — same as simplified path-loss model predictionVaries with distance and environment
Standard deviation (dB)σ_ψdBSpread of shadowing around the mean — larger = more random4–13 dB (outdoor), typically 6–10 dB urban
📊 What σ = 8 dB means: About 68% of receivers at distance d will have path loss within ±8 dB of the mean. So if the model predicts −90 dBm received power, roughly 68% of actual receivers are between −98 dBm and −82 dBm. Some unlucky receivers could be 16 dB below average — completely unusable without extra transmit power margin.

Step 5 — Shadowing Autocorrelation

Shadow fading is not completely independent from location to location. If two points are close together (e.g., 10 m apart), they likely have the same obstacles nearby — their shadowing is correlated. The correlation model:

A(δ) = σ_ψdB² · e^(−δ/Xc) Eq. (2.50) · δ = separation distance · Xc = decorrelation distance · at δ=Xc: correlation drops to 1/e ≈ 37%
EnvironmentDecorrelation distance Xc
Outdoor suburban macrocells (900 MHz)~50–100 m
Urban microcells (2 GHz)~10 m

For a user moving at velocity v, the time-domain decorrelation is obtained by substituting δ = v·τ.

Step 6 — How to Simulate Shadow Fading

Generate shadow fading with the correct correlation structure:

  • Generate white Gaussian noise with power σ²_ψdB
  • Pass through a first-order filter with response e^(−δ/Xc)
  • Output = correlated log-normal shadow fading process ψ_dB(d)

Practice — §2.7 Shadow Fading
  1. A wireless system has mean received power −95 dBm (from path-loss model) and shadow fading σ = 8 dB. (a) What range of powers contains 68% of receivers? (b) A receiver needs at least −105 dBm to work. What fraction of receivers succeed? (c) How much extra transmit power (fade margin) is needed to cover 95% of receivers?
  2. Why is it better to average path-loss measurements in dB rather than in linear (Watts)? Give a conceptual and a statistical reason.
  3. A user moves at 30 km/h through an environment with decorrelation distance Xc = 50 m. How long does it take in seconds for shadow fading to decorrelate? What about at 120 km/h?

1. (a) 68% within ±1σ = ±8 dB. Range: −95−8 to −95+8 = −103 to −87 dBm. (b) Need Pr ≥ −105 dBm, mean = −95, so need: current value ≥ −105, i.e., fading must not reduce by more than 10 dB below mean. P(ψ_dB ≤ 10 dB above mean) = P(X ≤ 10/8) = P(X ≤ 1.25) = Φ(1.25) ≈ 89.4% of receivers. (c) 95% coverage means need only 5% to fail: P(ψ_dB > margin) = 0.05, so margin = Φ⁻¹(0.95) × σ = 1.645 × 8 = 13.16 dB extra fade margin.

2. Conceptual: Shadow fading is log-normal, meaning its distribution is Gaussian in dB. Averaging in dB gives the mean of the underlying Gaussian distribution — the statistically meaningful parameter. Averaging in linear Watts gives the mean of the log-normal distribution, which is shifted from the dB median and is dominated by the largest outliers. Statistical: It minimises mean-squared error in the log domain, which is what the model is fit against.

3. At 30 km/h = 8.33 m/s: time to travel 50 m = 50/8.33 = 6.0 seconds. At 120 km/h = 33.33 m/s: time = 50/33.33 = 1.5 seconds. So fast-moving users experience independent fading much more rapidly — this affects handover algorithms and channel tracking.
